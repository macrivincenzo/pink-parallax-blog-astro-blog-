SDE Analysis for Dental Practices

Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) is the most accurate method for dental practice valuation. Unlike traditional businesses, dental practices have unique financial structures that require specialized analysis.

Dental Practice SDE Formula

SDE = Net Income + Owner's Salary + Benefits + Depreciation + Interest + Non-recurring Expenses + Owner's Personal Expenses

Key Dental Practice SDE Adjustments

Adjustment Category Description Typical Range
Owner's Compensation Fair market salary for dentist owner $200K - $400K+
Benefits & Perks Health insurance, retirement, car allowance $30K - $100K
Personal Expenses Personal items run through business $15K - $75K
Non-recurring Items One-time expenses, legal fees, renovations Varies

Dental Practice Valuation Methods

Professional dental practice appraisers use three primary valuation approaches:

1. Income Approach (Most Common)

This method focuses on the practice's ability to generate future earnings. It's the most relevant for dental practices because buyers are primarily interested in the income potential.

SDE Multiple Method

The most commonly used income approach method for dental practices:

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Projects future cash flows and discounts them to present value. More complex but provides detailed analysis of growth assumptions.

2. Market Approach

Compares your practice to similar dental practices that have sold recently. Limited by availability of comparable sales data in the dental industry.

3. Asset Approach

Values the practice based on its tangible and intangible assets. Less commonly used as the primary method but important for asset-heavy practices.

Step-by-Step Dental Practice Valuation Process

Phase 1: Financial Analysis (Days 1-3)

  1. Gather Financial Documents
    • 3 years of tax returns
    • 3 years of P&L statements
    • Current year P&L (month-to-date)
    • Balance sheet
  2. Calculate Seller's Discretionary Earnings
    • Start with net income
    • Add back owner compensation
    • Add back depreciation and amortization
    • Add back interest expense
    • Add back non-recurring expenses
    • Add back personal expenses

Phase 2: Operational Analysis (Days 4-6)

  1. Analyze Patient Base
    • Total active patients (18-month criteria)
    • Patient retention rates
    • New patient acquisition
    • Patient demographics and insurance mix
  2. Evaluate Operational Metrics
    • Production per hour
    • Collection efficiency
    • Appointment availability
    • Staff productivity metrics

Phase 3: Asset Assessment (Days 7-9)

  1. Equipment Valuation
    • Dental chairs and units
    • X-ray equipment and technology
    • Sterilization equipment
    • Computer systems and software
  2. Lease and Location Analysis
    • Lease terms and rates
    • Location demographics
    • Competition analysis
    • Parking and accessibility

Phase 4: Market Comparison (Days 10-12)

  1. Comparable Sales Research
    • Similar practice sales in area
    • Industry multiple analysis
    • Market trend evaluation
  2. Risk Assessment
    • Staff retention risk
    • Patient transition risk
    • Competition threats
    • Technology obsolescence

Phase 5: Valuation Conclusion (Days 13-15)

  1. Apply Valuation Methods
    • SDE multiple approach
    • Revenue multiple approach
    • Asset approach (if applicable)
  2. Final Valuation Range
    • Reconcile different approaches
    • Consider market conditions
    • Provide final value range
    • Identify value enhancement opportunities

Common Dental Practice Valuation Mistakes to Avoid

Financial Mistakes

  • Inadequate Record Keeping: Poor financial documentation reduces buyer confidence
  • Mixing Personal/Business Expenses: Makes SDE calculation difficult
  • Understating Owner Benefits: Reduces calculated SDE and practice value
  • Ignoring Market Trends: Not adjusting for current market conditions

Operational Mistakes

  • Neglecting Patient Retention: High turnover reduces practice value
  • Delaying Equipment Updates: Outdated technology impacts buyer appeal
  • Poor Staff Management: High turnover increases buyer risk
  • Weak Marketing: Limited new patient flow concerns buyers

Valuation Mistakes

  • Using Wrong Multiples: Applying general business multiples to dental practices
  • Ignoring Local Market: Not considering regional differences
  • Overvaluing Assets: Equipment often worth less than book value
  • Underestimating Risk: Not properly assessing transition risks

Frequently Asked Questions

What multiple should I expect for my dental practice?

Dental practice multiples typically range from 2.0x to 5.0x SDE, depending on practice type, location, and performance. General practices average 2.5-3.5x SDE, while specialty practices often achieve 3.0-5.0x SDE.

How do I calculate my practice's SDE?

Start with net income and add back owner's salary, benefits, depreciation, interest, and non-recurring expenses. Also add back any personal expenses run through the business.

What factors most impact dental practice value?

Key factors include patient base strength, location demographics, financial performance trends, equipment condition, staff stability, and growth potential.

How long does it take to sell a dental practice?

Dental practice sales typically take 6-12 months from listing to closing. Well-prepared practices with strong financials and operations often sell faster.

Should I update equipment before selling?

Strategic equipment updates can increase practice value, but over-investing right before sale may not provide full return. Focus on updates that significantly impact patient care and efficiency.

How do patient charts factor into valuation?

Active patient charts are valuable assets. Practices with 2,000+ active patients typically achieve higher multiples. Patient retention and new patient flow are key metrics.

What's the difference between asset sale and stock sale?

Most dental practice sales are asset sales, where the buyer purchases practice assets and assumes selected liabilities. Stock sales are less common and involve buying the corporation.

How do DSOs value practices differently?

DSOs often pay premium multiples but may have different evaluation criteria, focusing on scalability, technology integration, and strategic market position.
